# Chapter 1: Baseline

The apartment didn't need to be clean; it just needed to be predictable.

To John, the clutter in the corners of his living room wasn't "mess"—it was just unprioritized data. The stack of mail on the counter, the pile of laundry on the chair, the half-empty mugs in the sink; they were low-resolution clusters that didn't require his cognitive cycles. They existed in his peripheral vision, stable and static. He didn't need to interact with them to know they were there.

He preferred his mental architecture to be high-resolution.

He sat at his desk, the only surface in the apartment that followed a strict, logical geometry. The light from the window was dim, filtered through the thin grey curtains he had chosen specifically because they didn't create sharp, distracting shadows. 

On the screen, the code was beautiful. It was a series of nested functions, each one executing with mathematical certainty. There was no ambiguity here. No social subtext to decode, no micro-expressions to interpret, no "unstructured noise" to filter through. There was only the input, the process, and the output. It was the only place where the world actually made sense.

He took a sip of his coffee. It was exactly sixty-five degrees Celsius—the temperature he had calibrated for maximum thermal satisfaction without the distraction of heat.

He checked the time. 07:14.

The Sunday routine was a sequence of low-friction operations. He liked Sundays; the city felt less like a congested network and more like a series of discrete, manageable nodes. The commuters were fewer, the social bandwidth was lower, and the sensory load was at its weekly minimum.

He stood up, his movements practiced and efficient. He reached for his jacket—a charcoal grey, technical fabric. It was a neutral choice. It was designed to blend, to minimize his visual footprint. 

As he stepped out of the apartment and into the hallway, he felt the familiar, subtle engagement of his emulation layer. He adjusted his posture, softened his gaze, and prepared to navigate the world. The mask was on. The routine had begun.
